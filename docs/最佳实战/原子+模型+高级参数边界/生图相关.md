Image‑Generation Prompt Best Practices

This document presents a structured framework for writing prompts for image‑generation models. It applies to three major models currently available in the OpenCreator ecosystem: Banana Pro, Seedream 5.0 Lite and GPT Image 1.5. The goal is to provide reusable guidance for constructing prompts that yield high‑quality images while allowing the flexibility to adapt to individual creative needs.

1. Prompt Structure Template
A well‑formed image prompt should include the following key elements. Subject and environment details depend heavily on user context or visual references and therefore are not pre‑defined here. Instead, focus on the compositional and technical aspects that guide the model:
[Composition / Shot Type]        # e.g. rule of thirds, close‑up, wide shot
[Camera & Lens Details]          # e.g. DSLR with 35 mm lens, shallow depth of field
[Lighting & Mood]                # e.g. soft natural daylight, golden hour sunlight
[Visual Effects / Texture]       # e.g. film grain, dust particles, lens flare
[Style / Rendering]              # e.g. cinematic film look, fashion editorial, anime style
[Quality / Aspect Ratio Options] # e.g. 2K resolution, 4:3 aspect ratio

These elements can be rearranged or omitted depending on the scene. The critical point is to clearly communicate how the image should look and feel. According to Google’s prompt guide for Banana Pro, including details about composition, camera, lighting and style provides a strong baseline for high‑quality outputs[1].

2. Reusable Presets
Below are collections of preset phrases grouped by element. They are drawn from photography best practices and expert prompting guides[2][3]. Use them as building blocks when constructing prompts.

2.1 Composition / Shot Type
Rule of thirds – subject placed on a third grid line (left or right).
Eye‑level shot – camera at eye height for a natural perspective[4].
Close‑up – tight framing on the subject’s face or detail.
Medium shot – waist‑up framing; includes subject and some environment.
Wide shot – includes full subject and large portion of background; good for establishing context.
Candid moment – subject captured spontaneously in action or natural pose[4].
These settings influence the feeling of the image and help the model interpret composition correctly.

2.2 Camera & Lens
Camera and lens language pushes models toward photographic realism[3]. Useful phrases include:
DSLR / DSLR camera – emphasises professional photography quality.
35 mm lens – creates balanced, documentary‑style perspective[3].
50 mm lens – a natural focal length similar to human vision.
85 mm portrait lens – flattering for portraits, shallow depth of field.
Macro lens – emphasises small details at close distance.
Shallow depth of field – subject in sharp focus with blurred background (bokeh).
Telephoto lens – compresses distance and emphasises background elements.
Including camera or lens details tells the model to simulate real optical behaviour and improves realism[3].

2.3 Lighting & Mood
Lighting defines mood and atmosphere. Recommended phrases[5]:
Soft natural daylight – diffused sunlight; gentle shadows.
Golden hour sunlight – warm, low‑angle sunlight at sunrise or sunset[5].
High‑contrast cinematic lighting – strong directional light with deep shadows.
Low‑key moody lighting – dark backgrounds with minimal highlights.
Neon or cyberpunk lighting – saturated coloured lights; dramatic and stylised.
Studio lighting with softboxes – evenly lit; no harsh shadows.
Backlit silhouette – subject lit from behind; dramatic outline.
Lighting cues should align with the intended mood and environment.

2.4 Visual Effects / Texture
These effects add realism or stylistic flourishes:
Photorealistic texture – emphasises natural imperfections such as skin pores, fabric grain and dust[6].
Film grain / noise – adds grain to simulate analog film[7].
Dust particles / film dust – floating particles that add authenticity.
Motion blur – blur on moving parts; conveys dynamic motion.
Lens flare – streaks or flares from bright light sources.
Bokeh / out‑of‑focus highlights – blurred background lights.
Note: Texture can be crucial for UGC realism; small imperfections make outputs feel authentic[6].

2.5 Style / Rendering
Define the overall look and aesthetic:
Photorealistic / photographic realism – triggers realistic, real‑world rendering[2].
Cinematic film look – filmic colour grading and framing.
Vintage / analog – muted colours, film grain, retro tones.
Fashion editorial – high‑end stylised lighting and composition.
Anime / manga style – stylised line art and flat colours.
Illustration / digital painting – painted aesthetic; not photorealistic.
Minimalistic – clean, simple design with limited elements.
Use style keywords to push the model into a particular artistic domain.

2.6 Quality & Aspect Ratio
Choose resolution and aspect ratio appropriate for the output:
Resolution – 1K, 2K, 4K, depending on the model and desired detail. High resolutions produce sharper images but increase generation time and cost.
Aspect ratio – use common ratios like 1:1 (square), 3:2, 4:3, 16:9 or 21:9 depending on the target platform (social media post, banner, video cover). Banana Pro supports many ratios; GPT Image 1.5 has fixed size options (1024×1024, 1536×1024, 1024×1536).

3. Model‑Specific Guidelines
3.1 Banana Pro
Banana Pro excels at photorealistic and high‑fidelity images. To leverage its strengths:
Use realism triggers like “photorealistic”, “real‑world photography”, “cinematic realism”[2].
Include camera and lens details (35 mm lens, shallow depth of field) to simulate real optics[3].
Specify natural lighting or golden hour conditions[5].
Add texture and imperfections (skin pores, fabric grain) to improve authenticity[6].
Choose appropriate aspect ratio and resolution (1K/2K/4K). Higher resolution yields better detail but longer generation time.

3.2 Seedream 5.0 Lite
Seedream focuses on stylised, fashion‑forward and internet aesthetic images. Best practices:
Follow the formula Subject → Setting → Style → Lighting → Technical suggested by FAL AI to structure prompts[8].
Use descriptive, complete sentences rather than comma‑separated tags[9].
Emphasise creative styles (editorial, cyberpunk, neon lighting) and material textures.
Provide context about time of day, mood and style to guide the model’s multi‑step reasoning.
Consider using the num_images parameter to request multiple variations (up to 6).

3.3 GPT Image 1.5
GPT Image 1.5 delivers balanced, controllable outputs with simple parameter options. Tips:
Use camera and lighting language similar to Banana Pro (35 mm lens, soft natural daylight).
Include style descriptors such as “cinematic film look”, “minimalist design”, “flat graphic style”.
Avoid excessive complexity; GPT Image 1.5 prompts should be concise. Use the size and quality parameters to control output resolution and fidelity.
It supports transparent backgrounds for design assets; specify transparent if needed.

4. UGC & Photorealistic Adaptation
User‑generated content (UGC) often aims for authenticity over perfection. To evoke real‑world photography:
Add realism triggers: include words like photorealistic or real‑world photography to push models toward realistic renderings[2].
Embrace imperfections: mention small flaws (skin pores, stray hairs, wrinkles, dust) and texture to avoid plastic or cartoon‑like results[6].
Use natural lighting: prefer soft daylight, golden hour or low‑key studio lighting; avoid “fantasy” or unrealistic light sources[5].
Select candid composition: request candid moments or off‑center framing to mimic real photography[4].
Introduce film grain and noise: subtle grain helps make images feel less artificial[7].
Avoid unrealistic elements: use negative prompts to avoid cartoon style, plastic skin or over‑saturated colours[10].
